lived experiences project title

What is statistics? Different people give different answers. A college student whose major is not mathematics, natural science, or engineering related, might say that statistics is a study of means of summarizing collections of data, or just a study of data. A natural science major student, such as a biology student, might give us a more interesting definition: “Statistics is a relative of mathematics. Although statistics and mathematics are kin, looking closely we see differences in their DNA.”

In fact, we do not need to argue which definition is better, since statistics is related to, and applied to, many fields of study. I believe statistics is either a science studying data and uncertainty theoretically, or a tool to solve problems from many fields by helping us correctly collect and interpret data. Although I am officially a double major in economics and mathematics, I consider myself a student of statistics.

Like almost every kids, I love video games. Coincidentally, the story between me and Statistics starts with video games. Almost 10 years ago, I came across a soccer video game called Football Manager. It is a series of soccer management simulation games based on a massive database of real-world players and clubs from around the world, in which you play as the head coach or manager dealing with tactics, transfers, trainings, soccer games, media, etc. Playing the game is just as in the real world, every unremarkable change is possibly associated with the success or failure of your “career”. Let me give you some examples, a right comment made to your players during halftime can change the result of the game significantly, but a wrong plan of training will injure your players before the game. In addition to your team’s performance, you also have to maintain good relations with either fans of club or your colleagues and players, to keep your job as a manager. If you are doing well, you will receive offers form more famous club with higher salaries, or even the honor to serve for your country – the national team. I bought almost every year’s version of this game as almost every team’s roster changes a lot. What made me so interested in this game is its precise simulation of ten thousand players from about 50 countries. Also, this game taught me one thing that elements (or variables in mathematical sense) from different fields have something to do with each other. This game has even been recognized by many real-life soccer clubs as a credible means of scouting opponents and players. Being a soccer fan and playing Football Manager is basically how I became interested in statistics, although I knew little about the science behind the video game.

As a soccer fan, I frequently browse the Internet for soccer related news or programs. In 2009, I watched a sports program reviewing the best 10 goals of all time in the FIFA World Cup. One of those goals was scored by Argentina against Serbia-Montenegro at the group stage of the 2006 FIFA World Cup. The offensive was launched by Argentina’s defender after an excellent tackle and the goal was finished, after 26 consecutive perfect passes between 8 players, from Argentina's backfield to Serbia-Montenegro’s penalty area, by Esteban Cambiasso. I remembered that this spectacular goal was described by the television host as the best goal scored ever in the history of FIFA World Cup. In order to emphasize the significance of the goal, the host stated that the goal had even become a subject of statistical research at university. The host’s comment really impressed me, since at that time I was, like other high school graduates, facing the choice of college major. Doing research on soccer in a scientific way seems to be very interesting for me. I did some Internet searching for more information about the research mentioned by the host. Luckily, I found a research publication on Argentina’s soccer team in the 2006 World Cup, A Potential Function Approach to the Flow of Play in Soccer, by David R. Brillinger, a professor of Statistics at University of California, Berkeley. Although scientific terminology and complicated mathematical expressions constituted a huge barrier between me and the research, and even today the whole research is still beyond the extent of my knowledge, I was impressed by some of the context noted by Professor Brillinger. In the abstract, Professor Brillinger mentioned that the result of the research is a potential function which can be used for prediction, simulation, or other research regarding a soccer team’s playing. The abstract surprised and confused me. Surprisingly, I saw an answer to how Football Manager might simulate soccer games so closely. What confused me was how things like team chemistry could be quantified and then interpreted as function. Based on my knowledge then, it seemed that only numerable or at least measurable subjects can be quantified, for example, en average SAT scores, or miles per gallon of gas used by an automobile. Things like team chemistry, or players’ performance by instincts or experience, seemed impossible to measure via data analysis. But, I also had another idea: If the technology of interpreting various things by data does work, learning it will enable me to get a job in a variety of fields. With that thought, I applied to college with a major of statistics.

In my freshman year, I started with fundamental math courses like calculus and I also kept looking for courses covering knowledge similar to that used in Professor Brillinger’s research. Thanks to Professor Brillinger’s putting some keywords of his research in the front page, I can trace some of the rationale by terms like exploratory data analysis and regression model. Unfortunately, related courses provided by Department of Statistics are graduate level, for instance, STT 863 Statistical Methods I, which even has a graduate level course as its prerequisite. Although I had already decided to apply to graduate school, I was still somewhat disappointed to know the courses enrollment constraints.

You never know what’s coming for you. Due to my desire to become an Associate of the Society of Actuaries, last fall semester I took an Economics course taught by Professor Tim Vogelsang, to fulfill the requirement “validation by educational experience of economics. Unlike other undergraduate economics courses, which are dominated by theoretical knowledge without any mathematics and statistics involved, this course introduces a statistical method called econometrics. Since the econometrics model uses the principles of a regression model, the econometrics courses was a very desirable way for me to approach gaining greater knowledge of statistics. Taking the course also got me more interested in studying Economics. As the course progressed, I began to know that, unlike in the period of Adam Smith, today’s study of Economics requires plenty of quantitative analysis. Therefore, many contemporary economists have extremely strong mathematical and statistical backgrounds. The Department of Economics at Michigan State University provides a good example: Professor Tim Vogelsang, the Frederick S. Addy Distinguished Professor of Economics, majored in Mathematics at MIT, and Professor Gary Solon, previously serving as department chair at University of Michigan, was statistics major undergraduate student at Harvard.

In addition, after consulting with advisor of Department of Economics, I understood that being an economics major student allows me more opportunities of taking statistical related courses, within the range of 120 credits. So, I changed my major to economics, and now I enjoy learning statistics from a broader perspective.
