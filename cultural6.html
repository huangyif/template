Anticipated Effects

Social networking can either improve or impede the development of China. Although wrongdoings like fabricating facts and slandering people are within the scope of application of the criminal law, the current Criminal Law in China does not provide a clear idea about how to administer punishment for Internet cases. Article 246 of the Criminal Law of the People’s Republic of China stated: “Whoever, by means of violence or by other means, insults in public another person for fabricates facts to slander another person shall, if the circumstances are serious, be sentenced to fixed-term imprisonment of not more than three years, criminal detention, public surveillance or deprivation of political rights.” Since there is no previous case about “serious circumstances” of internet wrongdoing, a discussion has ensued about how to apply criminal law to Internet wrongdoings. Every individual has the right to express his or her opinion on other individual. Sometimes, a sports fan might post insulting words regarding players of his host team solely because of the team’s terrible performance. Should a case like that be considered a serious circumstance of slandering? In order to respond to the public concern, the Supreme Court and Supreme Procuratorate made an official statement that gives several definitions of “serious circumstances” on the internet. For example, a slandering post which is reposted more than 500 times or has more than 5000 hits is considered “serious”. A post that cause insanity or suicide of victims or their relatives is considered “serious”. Although these definitions are at a tentative stage, it is very important to prevent the internet from hurting individuals, while maintaining social networking’s merit as a place of expressing and exchanging ideas and opinions.
